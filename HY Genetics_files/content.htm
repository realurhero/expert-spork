  1. A 4-year-old boy is brought to the physician because of a chronic cough that is worse at night and productive of thick white sputum. He is at the 3rd percentile for weight and height. Physical examination shows clubbing of the fingers. Chest auscultation shows diffuse inspiratory crackles and hyperresonance. The patient has a healthy 6-year-old sister. Which of the following represents the likelihood of this patient’s sister being a carrier of this condition?


    1. 1 of 1

    2. 1 of 2

    3. 1 of 3

    4. 1 of 4

    5. 2 of 3

    6. There is no chance the patient’s sister is a carrier

      The correct answer is E.


      Cystic fibrosis is an autosomal recessive condition on chromosome 7. There is a one-half chance each parent will pass his or her mutated allele to the offspring. In the following Punnett square, “A” refers to a non-diseased wild-type allele; “a” refers to a diseased allele.


      Since the sister is healthy (i.e., phenotypically normal), we know she can’t possibly be “aa,” so we can eliminate that. This leaves us with only three possibilities: AA + 2(Aa).


      2(Aa) / 3 total possibilities = 2/3 chance of being a carrier.


      Bottom line: Phenotypically normal siblings of patients with AR disorders bear a 2/3 chance of being a carrier. This is an important genetics ratio you need to memorize for USMLE, as the exam will often ask harder questions, where knowing this ratio is a mere component of, rather than answer to, the question.

  2. A 34-year-old woman presents to preconception counseling with her husband. Her family history is remarkable for a brother who died at the age of 3 from deficiency of a-L-iduronidase. Her husband’s family history is unremarkable. If the frequency of disease in the general population is approximately 1 in 100,000 for this condition, what is the probability this couple will have a son with the disease?


    A) 1/300

    B) 1/900

    C) 1/1900

    D) 1/4200

    E) 1/7600

    The correct answer is C.


    First solve for carrier frequency in general population (husband’s carrier risk):


    Hardy-Weinberg equation: 1 = p2 + 2pq + q2, where p2 is non-disease frequency (i.e., 1 – 1/100,000 99,999/100,000 ≈ 1), q2 is disease frequency (i.e., 1/100,000), and 2pq is carrier frequency.


    q2 = 1/100,000


    q = ✓1/100,000 = 1/316.2


    2pq = 2 x 1 x 1/316.2 = 1/158.1 = carrier frequency in general population Next solve for chance the patient (mom) is a carrier):

    Hurler syndrome is an AR disorder. As per question #1, there is a 2/3 a phenotypically normal patient is a carrier of an AR disorder if he or she has a sibling with the disorder.



    Now solve for the risk this couple would have a child who has the AR disease:


    2/3 (chance mom is carrier) x 1/158.1 (chance dad is carrier) x 1/4 (chance of aa) in Punnett square = 2/1897.2


    However the question asked what is the chance the couple will have a son with the disease, so now we multiply by 1/2 (chance of having a son). This would be the same for daughters because this is an AR disorder.


    So 2/1897.2 x 2 = 1/1897.2


    The USMLE will often give you conditions where q2 is, e.g., 1/10,000, so q is easier to solve for (1/100), without the need for decimals.

  3. An 8-year-old boy is brought to the physician by his mom for yellow discoloration of his eyes she first noticed this morning. He recently recovered from an upper respiratory tract infection. Serum studies show:


    Hemoglobin 10.3 g/dL

    Hematocrit 30.9% Total bilirubin 4.0 mg/dL Direct bilirubin 0.3 mg/dL


    A peripheral blood smear is shown:



    The boy’s parents would like to have two more children. What is the probability that both children will carry the same diagnosis?


    1. 1 of 1

    2. 1 of 2

    3. 1 of 4

    4. 1 of 8

    5. 1 of 16

    6. 1 of 32

    7. 1 of 64

    8. Cannot be determined as this condition is usually sporadic de novo

      The correct answer is E.


      The diagnosis is glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency, an X-linked recessive disorder.


      Unless in the rare event of heavily skewed lyonization (X-inactivation), only boys will have the disorder.


      The patient has hemolysis precipitated by viral infection. The smear shows Heinz bodies (oxidized, precipitated hemoglobin) and bite cells (degmacytes; partially phagocytosed RBCs).


      The mother must be a carrier for the disorder (i.e., she has one X chromosome with the mutation).


      The chance of her having another son is 1/2. The chance of her passing the mutation-laden X- chromosome to this son is 1/2. So the chance of having another son with the disease is 1/4.


      The chance therefore of two sons in a row with the disease would be 1/4 x 1/4 = 1/16 = the chance this couple’s next two children will have G6PD deficiency.

  4. A newborn male dies shortly after birth due to multiple organ system abnormalities. Autopsy shows an apparent transformation of lumbar vertebrae to a thoracic identity. Which of the following mechanisms relating to HOX gene may best explain this observation at the thoracic level?


    1. Alteration in transcription factor binding sites at the thoracic-lumbar vertebral junction

    2. Expression of a HOX gene normally transcribed only caudally

    3. Expression of a HOX gene normally transcribed only cranially

    4. Insufficient expression of a HOX gene at the lumbar level

    5. Insufficient expression of a HOX enhancer sequence at the thoracic level

      The correct answer is C.


      HOX genes are involved in the patterning of an embryo along the head-tail axis – i.e., they encode the characteristic positions of body parts, ensuring development at correct locations.


      For example, lumbar vertebrae develop specifically at the lumbar level, and not at the sacral, thoracic, or cervical levels, because HOX genes ensure this specific type of developmental patterning.


      If lumbar vertebrae are transformed into a thoracic identity – i.e., if vertebrae at the lumbar location have developed to appear like thoracic ones – then one possible mechanism is that thoracic HOX genes were inadvertently expressed at the lumbar location.


      Since thoracic HOX genes are cranial to the lumbar ones, the transformation of lumbar vertebrae into thoracic ones means that genes normally expressed only superior (cranial) got expressed at an inferior (caudal) location.


      Choice B would refer to the transformation of thoracic vertebrae into lumbar ones.

  5. A 42-year-old woman presents to the physician with a 3-month history of progressive shortness of breath on exertion. She has a history of recurrent epistaxis since childhood. Echocardiogram shows an ejection fraction of 75%. A photograph of the patient’s tongue is shown.



    The patient’s husband does not have a family history of the condition. What is the probability the patient and her husband will have a daughter who is a carrier?


    1. 0

    2. 1 of 1

    3. 1 of 2

    4. 1 of 3

    5. 1 of 4

    6. 1 of 6

    7. 1 of 8

    8. Cannot be determined without knowing the husband’s carrier status

      The correct answer is A.


      The diagnosis is hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia (Osler-Weber-Rendu), an autosomal dominant disorder.


      The image shown is a telangiectasia of the tongue. The USMLE is also known to show an image of nailbed telangiectasias.


      This patient has high-output cardiac failure secondary to a pulmonary arteriovenous fistula, which can present in adulthood. Telangiectasias of the nasal mucosa resulting in recurrent epistaxis since childhood are characteristic. Telangiectasias within the GI tract can cause GI bleeding and anemia.


      Autosomal dominant disorders do not have a carrier state, since only one mutation results in disease phenotype. Carrier states are only possible in AR and XR disorders.


      Even if a disease has incomplete penetrance (i.e., not all patients with diseased genotype bear the diseased phenotype), a patient with a dominant mutation would still not be considered a carrier. He or she would be said to have an AD disorder, albeit with reduced penetrance.


      The husband cannot be a carrier, since HHT is an AD disorder and he does not have family history.

  6. A 39-year-old man and his wife have two sons with history of retinoblastoma and a daughter with osteosarcoma. Neither the patient nor his wife have history of health problems. The patient is identified as II-5 on the pedigree.




    Which of the following best explains the reason for the patient’s health?


    1. Germline mosaicism

    2. Heteroplasmy

    3. Incomplete penetrance

    4. Somatic mosaicism

    5. Variable expressivity

      The correct answer is C.




      This patient’s (II-5) family has congenital retinoblastoma. Retinoblastoma in infancy and/or osteosarcoma later in life are most characteristic of the condition.


      Congenital retinoblastoma is an autosomal dominant disorder with incomplete penetrance.


      Incomplete penetrance is when not everyone with the diseased genotype bears the diseased phenotype (i.e., not everyone with the disease expresses it); this is sometimes referred to as “skipping a generation,” and also applies to the BRCA genes.


      Variable expressivity, in contrast, implies 100% penetrance – i.e., everyone with the disease expresses it in some form. The difference is the mere severity in which family members manifest the condition. NF1 is a classic example, where everyone with the disease has it, but whilst one family member might only present with axillary and groin freckling, another might develop pheochromocytoma and optic glioma. If the NBME/USMLE gives you a pedigree that looks like incomplete penetrance (as with this question), but the diagnosis in the family is NF1, choose variable expressivity, not incomplete penetrance. A patient with minimal disease severity in NF1 might not report having it.


      Heteroplasmy is a phenomenon seen in mitochondrial disorders, an inheritance pattern characterized by strict maternal transmission (i.e., mothers transmit disease to sons and daughters; fathers never transmit disease), where varying amounts of diseased mitochondria are passed to the next generation, resulting in offspring with disease of varying severity. This is due to the mother’s oocytes carrying thousands of copies of mitochondrial DNA, where the oocytes differ in the ratios of diseased mitochondria they contain. Do not confuse this with variable expressivity, which instead refers to

      non-mitochondrial disorders, where a chromosomal mutation can result in varying severity of disease.


      Somatic mosaicism is when only a percentage of an individual’s “body cells” (somatic = body) contain a diseased genotype. This results from a post-fertilization mitotic error, where the mutation results in only some of the organism’s cells being affected by disease. Classic examples for USMLE would be mosaic Turner or Down, where the answer is “post-fertilization mitotic error.” Sturge- Weber is also an example of somatic mosaicism.


      Germline (gonadal) mosaicism is when the spermatogonia or oogonia of one of the parents carry a mutation, but the somatic (body) cells do not. The result is that offspring can have an AD disorder despite the parents not being affected. Germline mosaicism is the answer classically for achondroplasia – i.e., the parents are normal but the child has the disease. The child will have 100% of his or her body cells affected because the original mutation was in the spermatogonia or oogonia of the parent, rather than occurring post-mitotically, as with somatic mosaicism. If the USMLE does not give achondroplasia, they will say two phenotypically normal parents have two children with an AD disorder, implying there is a germline mutation in one of the parents (usually the father).

  7. A 2-year-old boy is brought to the pediatrician by his parents for a follow-up appointment. He has a history since birth of recurrent Staph, Candida, and viral infections. Examination shows scanty lymph nodes and tonsils and absent thymic shadow. Molecular analysis shows deficiency of adenosine deaminase. The boy’s parents are planning to have more children. What is the probability that they will have a daughter with this molecular defect?


    1. Zero percent, since this condition is X-linked recessive

    2. 1 in 2, since this condition is autosomal dominant

    3. 1 in 2, since this condition is autosomal recessive

    4. 1 in 4, since this condition is autosomal dominant

    5. 1 in 4, since this condition is autosomal recessive

    6. 1 in 8, since this condition is autosomal dominant

    7. 1 in 8, since this condition is autosomal recessive

    8. 2 in 3, since this condition is autosomal dominant

    9. 2 in 3, since this condition is autosomal recessive

      The correct answer is G.


      The diagnosis is severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID). There are two main types of SCID:


      • XR: common gamma chain mutation, or IL-2 receptor deficiency

      • AR: adenosine deaminase (ADA) deficiency


      Since the patient has ADA deficiency, we know he has the AR, not XR, type of SCID.


      This means there is a 1/4 chance the couple will have another child with AR SCID, and a 1/8 chance they will have a daughter with AR SCID (i.e., 1/4 chance of next child with SCID x 1/2 chance of having a daughter).


      The ratio 2/3 refers to the probability that a phenotypically normal sibling of a patient with an AR disorder is a carrier for the disease.


      If the patient in the vignette had common gamma chain mutation or IL-2 receptor deficiency (both XR SCID), then the answer would be zero percent.

  8. A child (III-1 in the pedigree) has a history of a condition characterized by self-mutilation, aggressive behavior, and orange-red, sandy crystals in the diaper.



    Assuming the mating partner of II-5 is genotypically normal, what is the probability that individual III-4 develops a similar condition?


    1. Zero percent

    2. 1 in 2

    3. 1 in 4

    4. 1 in 8

    5. 2 in 3

    6. Cannot be determined without carrier status of mating partner of II-5

      The correct answer is A.


      The diagnosis is Lesch-Nyhan syndrome, an X-linked recessive disorder, caused by deficiency of the enzyme hypoxanthine-guanine phosphoribosyltransferase (HGPRT). This enzyme is required for the recycling of purines. Deficiency leads to gout due to overproduction of uric acid. Vignettes will give young boys who exhibit self-mutilation and red-orange sand-like crystals in their diapers.


      XR diseases cannot be transmitted from father to son, so even if II-5 in the pedigree had the condition, he wouldn’t be able to transfer it anyway.


      If we consider daughter III-3, there is also a zero percent chance she would be a carrier for the condition as well, since II-5 does not have the disease.

  9. A 54-year-old woman (II-1) presents to the cardiologist for a follow-up examination. She has an ejection fraction of 35% and a dilated cardiac silhouette. She has chronic joint pain of her hands and an occasional sore knee managed with indomethacin. Fasting blood glucose is 130 mg/dL.



    If the disease frequency of this condition is 1/400 in the general population, what is the probability that III-1 has the disease?


    A) 3/200

    B) 3/400

    C) 8/305

    D) 15/304

    E) 19/1200

    F) 21/1559

    The correct answer is E.


    The diagnosis is hereditary hemochromatosis, an autosomal recessive condition, caused by mutations in the HFE gene on chromosome 6. This results in iron overload caused by an inability to shut off iron absorption in the duodenum.


    Since individual II-1 has the disease, we know that I-1 is a carrier. In addition, we also know that phenotypically normal siblings of individuals with AR disorders have a 2/3 chance of being a carrier, since “aa” is eliminated from the Punnett square (because they don’t have the disease). This means there is a 2/3 chance II-5 is a carrier.


    Now we need to calculate the probability that the mating partner of II-5 is a carrier. Using our annoying as fuck Hardy-Weinberg equation:


    1 = p2 + 2pq + q2, where q2 = 1/400, and q = 1/20. Since 1 = p + q, p = 19/20.


    2pq (carrier frequency) therefore = 2(19/20)(1/20) = 38/400 = 19/200 in general population. Now we calculate the chance that III-1 has the disease:

    2/3 (chance II-5 father is carrier) x 19/200 (chance mother is carrier from general population) x 1/4 chance of “aa” within the Punnett square = 38/2400 = 19/1200.


    “Bronze” diabetes is a classic presentation for this condition, characterized by hyperpigmentation of the skin (due to hemosiderin deposition), diabetes mellitus (due to iron deposition in the tail of the pancreas), and various other findings, such as pseudogout (calcium pyrophosphate deposition disease), cardiomyopathy (dilated or restrictive), and infertility (due to hypothalamic or testicular iron deposition).


    This presentation is slightly harder because hyperpigmentation is omitted from the stem, but the patient has dilated cardiomyopathy, pseudogout, and diabetes mellitus.


    The two biggest risk factors for pseudogout are hereditary hemochromatosis and primary hyperparathyroidism. Pseudogout usually presents as either a monoarthritis of a large joint, such as the knee, or as a polyarthritis resembling osteoarthritis (OA). If the vignette gives you, e.g., Bouchard or Heberden nodes in a patient with hemochromatosis or primary hyperparathyroidism, the diagnosis is pseudogout, not OA.


    Some students will ask, “But if we are forced to choose which cardiomyopathy it is, dilated or restrictive, which one is it?” The answer is: whichever one they give you, since the presentations are disparate. DCM presents with S3, enlarged silhouette, and decreased ejection fraction. RCM presents with S4, JVD, a normal silhouette, and normal ejection fraction.

  10. A 36-year-old woman with HIV is undergoes HLA screening prior to commencement of abacavir as part of her anti-retroviral therapy. Genetic analysis confirms the homozygous presence of HLA B*5701 alleles. What is the likelihood this patient’s brother is also homozygous for the same alleles?


    1. Impossible to determine since HLA inheritance is polygenic

    2. 0%

      C) 25%

      D) 33%

      E) 50%

      F) 67%

      G) 75%

      H) 100%

      The correct answer is C.


      HLA genes are inherited as haplotypes, which are a set of alleles inherited from each parent. Each parent will have two haplotypes.



      The HLA B*5701 allele would be carried within one of the haplotypes of each parent. The patient is homozygous for the HLA B*5701 allele. This means she has the haplotype 1 / haplotype 3 combination. Since there are only four combinations, there is a 1/4 (25%) chance her brother would also have homozygous HLA B*5701 alleles.


      Abacavir is known to a cause a hypersensitivity-like reaction in patients with HLA B*5701 positivity. Screening for this HLA haplotype is required prior to commencement of abacavir.

  11. A 21-year-old woman presents to the physician for a two-month history of weakness, headache, and blurry vision. Serum studies show a hemoglobin of 18.5 g/dL. Serum calcium is 10.6 mg/dL. Urinalysis shows 1+ RBCs. CT of the abdomen confirms the diagnosis. In addition, cysts are seen within the pancreas. Which of the following is the most likely mode of inheritance of this condition?


    1. Autosomal dominant

    2. Autosomal recessive

    3. Mitochondrial

    4. X-linked dominant

    5. X-linked recessive

      The correct answer is A.


      The diagnosis is von Hippel-Lindau (VHL), an autosomal dominant disorder on chromosome 3, which results in failure of degradation of hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF).


      VHL is characterized by renal cell carcinoma (can become bilateral), pancreatic cysts, and cerebellar and retinal hemangioblastomas.


      Renal cell carcinoma can present with polycythemia (due to EPO secretion) and hypercalcemia (paraneoplastic PTHrp secretion; similar to squamous cell carcinoma of the lung, yes).


      Blurry vision and headache can be a result of hyperviscosity syndrome due to the high hemoglobin. The former could also be seen in retinal hemangioblastoma.


      VHL is one of the phakomatoses (neurocutaneous disorders). The other high-yield ones for USMLE are neurofibromatosis 1 and 2 (NF1, NF2), tuberous sclerosis (TSC), and Sturge-Weber.


      NF1, NF2, TSC, and VHL are all autosomal dominant. Sturge-Weber is not inherited and is a somatic mosaicism disorder (post-fertilization mitotic error).


      NF1 (chromosome 17) is characterized by neurofibromas, axillary and groin freckling, café au lait spots (hyperpigmented macules), pheochromocytoma, optic glioma, and “weird” CNS tumors such as oligodendroglioma and ependymoma.


      NF2 (chromosome 22) presents as acoustic schwannoma (can be bilateral) and meningioma.


      TSC (chromosomes 9 and 16; coding for hamartin and tuberin proteins, respectively) presents as periventricular nodules (tubers), adenoma sebaceum (angiofibromas; reddish-peach-colored papules in the nasolabial folds and on the bridge of the nose), cardiac rhabdomyoma, subungual fibromas, lymphangioleiomyomatosis, renal angiomyolipoma, ashleaf spots (hypopigmented macules), and shagreen patches (hyperpigmented velvety lesions). Vignettes will often be children presenting with seizures (i.e., writing movements during sleep) due to the tubers.


      Sturge-Weber presents with Port wine stain birthmark (nevus flammeus; but can also present as violaceous papules in a temporal distribution), glaucoma, leptomeningeal angiomas, and seizures.

  12. A 22-year-old woman presents to the physician for a two-month history of fatigue, mild cognitive degeneration, and clumsiness. Physical examination shows scleral icterus and a resting tremor of her left hand. Serum studies show elevated hepatic transaminases. Hemoglobin is 9.5 g/dL. Blood smear shows spherocytes. Which of the following is the most likely mode of inheritance of this condition?


    1. Autosomal dominant

    2. Autosomal recessive

    3. Mitochondrial

    4. X-linked dominant

    5. X-linked recessive

      The correct answer is B.


      The diagnosis is Wilson disease, an autosomal recessive condition on chromosome 13, characterized by an inability to excrete copper into bile and resultant copper overload.


      Patients have increased urinary copper and decreased serum ceruloplasmin (copper-binding protein). The latter results in increased free serum copper that accumulates within RBCs, leading to hemolysis and spherocytosis.


      Spherocytes are not limited to hereditary spherocytosis (which is autosomal dominant). The USMLE wants you to know they are frequently seen in the setting of drug- and infection-induced hemolysis. They are also seen in hemolytic anemia in Wilson disease. (Should be noted tangentially that they are not seen in G6PD deficiency; bite cells are seen instead)


      Wilson disease can present as Parkinsonism in a young patient (i.e., unilateral resting tremor, bradykinesia, shuffling gait, etc.), and hence is known as a Parkinson-plus disorder (i.e., appears like Parkinson disease but is not). This is due to copper deposition within the basal ganglia. Other HY Parkinson-plus syndromes for USMLE are progressive supranuclear palsy, normal pressure hydrocephalus, Lewy body dementia, MPTP-induced Parkinsonism, and antipsychotic-induced Parkinsonism.


      Liver disease and cirrhosis are also important findings in Wilson. LFTs may be variable depending on stage of liver failure. Cirrhotic patients will have elevated prothrombin time (PT).


      Kayser-Fleischer rings are often too easy and omitted from USMLE vignettes. These are copper rings seen with Descemet membrane of the cornea. The USMLE might give you a vignette of Wilson disease, and then the answer is, “Perform a slit-lamp examination.”


      Treatment of Wilson disease is with penicillamine, a copper chelator. Trientine and zinc supplements may also be used.


      Bottom line: The heavy metal disorders – Wilson and hemochromatosis – are autosomal recessive.

  13. A 15-year-old boy has a 4-month history of progressive shortness of breath with exertion, muscle weakness, and worsening exercise tolerance. Vitals are within normal limits. Physical examination shows bilateral ptosis. Muscle strength is 3/5 in all extremities. A pedigree is shown. The patient is represented by III-10.



    Which of the following best explains the combination of findings in this patient?


    1. Allelic heterogeneity

    2. Anticipation

    3. Chimerism

    4. Complete penetrance

    5. Heteroplasmy

    6. Pleiotropy

      The correct answer is E.


      This patient has a mitochondrial disorder. This is evidenced by the classic constellation of lactic acidosis, hypotonia, and ear and/or eye problem.


      Mitochondrial inheritance is strictly maternal – i.e., only females can pass disease to offspring; males cannot transmit disease.


      This is because the mitochondrial genes are separate from the chromosomal ones. The oocytes of the female contain thousands of copies mitochondrial genes, which are the source of mitochondrial DNA in the resultant zygote. The sperm does not contribute mitochondrial DNA to the zygote.


      Each oocyte contains a different proportion/fraction of diseased mitochondria. Depending on the fraction of diseased versus normal mitochondrial DNA, the resultant offspring will bear varying disease severity. This is referred to as heteroplasmy.


      Allelic heterogeneity refers to different alleles resulting in the same disease. Cystic fibrosis is a classic example, where a large number of possible mutations within the CFTR gene are known to cause disease, with �F508 being most common. The Sweat chloride test is more accurate than genotyping for CF because common CFTR screening panels will only detect upwards of 96% of likely mutations. Sweat chloride test, however, will be positive in all patients with CF.


      Anticipation refers to the propensity for trinucleotide repeat (TNR) disorders presenting earlier and more severe with each successive generation. This is due to an expansion of the number of repeats with each generation. For example, Huntington disease may present in a male at age 36 who has 55 repeats, whereas in his mother it presented at age 44, and she only had 48 repeats (40 or more CAG cause disease).


      Chimerism is a phenomenon seen in some animal species, i.e., mice and cats, where more than two parents contribute DNA to the offspring. This is due to fusion of embryos. Chimerism is not seen in humans.


      Complete penetrance is when 100% of those with a diseased genotype go on to develop the diseased phenotype. Incomplete penetrance is “skipping a generation,” where some individuals with a diseased genotype do not develop the diseased phenotype. Hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia, congenital retinoblastoma, and BRCA mutations are classic examples of incomplete penetrance.


      Pleiotropy is when one gene results in many phenotypic effects – e.g., TP53 mutations resulting in Li- Fraumeni syndrome can result in many different types of cancers (e.g., pancreatic, colon). Another example is phenylketonuria (PKU), where a mutation in phenylalanine hydroxylase can result in partial albinism, mental retardation, and musty body odor.

  14. A 24-year-old woman presents to the physician with an ongoing history of heavy menses and epistaxis. Serum studies show:


    Platelet count: 200,000/µL Platelet aggregation studies: Normal Prothrombin time: 12 seconds Partial thromboplastin time: 38 seconds Bleeding time: 9 minutes

    Ristocetin assay: Suppressed


    Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?


    1. Glanzmann thrombasthenia

    2. Hemophilia A

    3. Hemophilia B

    4. Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura

    5. Vitamin K deficiency

    6. Von Willebrand disease

      The correct answer is F.


      Student says, “Wait, how is this a genetics question. I’m confused.” The USMLE will sometimes weave genetics knowledge into questions rather than asking you for inheritance patterns directly. This type of question is one of the highest yield applications of inheritance patterns.


      Von Willebrand disease (vWD) is autosomal dominant. Hemophilia A and B are both X-linked recessive. Since this vignette is a female patient, even if you aren’t sure of the hematologic diagnosis, if you know the inheritance patterns, you can instantly eliminate the hemophilias because the patient is not male.


      Von Willebrand disease will almost always present as a combination of one clotting factor problem (i.e., menorrhagia, excessive bleeding with tooth extraction) and one platelet problem (petechiae, purpura, ecchymoses, epistaxis). If the vignette unexpectedly gives you, e.g., only menorrhagia, you will be able to diagnose definitively based on the laboratory findings.


      Von Willebrand factor (vWF) helps enable platelet adhesion by bridging glycoprotein Ib on platelets to vascular endothelium. The ristocetin assay will be abnormal in patients with impaired platelet adhesion. This means vWD and Bernard-Soulier disease (deficiency of GpIb) both have abnormal ristocetin assays.


      Von Willebrand factor plays no role in platelet aggregation, where Gp IIb/IIIa on adjacent platelets are bridged together by fibrinogen. This means platelet aggregation studies are normal in vWD.


      Bleeding time (normally 2-7 minutes) is increased if there is a platelet problem. Since platelet adhesion is disrupted in vWD, bleeding time is always increased.


      Von Willebrand factor has an auxiliary role in helping to stabilize factor VIII in plasma. This means about half the time partial thromboplastin time (aPTT) will be increased. Normal range is 25-40 seconds. Since this stabilization of factor VIII is not the primary role of vWF, aPTT is normal in about half of USMLE questions. Students normally fuck this up, as they erroneously think aPTT is always increased in addition to BT. But aPTT is increased in only about half of questions.


      Prothrombin time (PT) is normal in vWD because factor VIII is in the intrinsic, not the extrinsic, pathway.


      Platelet count is normal in vWD because there is no mechanism for platelet destruction. There is merely impaired binding of Gp Ib on platelets to vascular endothelium.


      Hemophilia A and B are deficiency of factors VIII and IX, respectively. The textbook vignette will be a school-age male with hemarthrosis. aPTT will be elevated. BT and PT are normal.


      Glanzmann thrombasthenia is deficiency of glycoprotein IIb/IIIa on platelets, resulting in impaired aggregation. The ristocetin assay is normal in Glanzmann thrombasthenia.


      Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP; aka immune thrombocytopenia purpura) is an isolated thrombocytopenia caused by antibodies against Gp IIb/IIIa on platelets. The classic presentation is a school-age kid or a woman 30s-40s with epistaxis or random bruising. Only BT is increased, and platelet count is always down.


      Vitamin K deficiency presents with normal bleeding time, and increased PT and aPTT. Vitamin K does not affect platelets. It is a cofactor the for the enzyme gamma-glutamyl carboxylase. Deficiency leads to impaired clotting due to inability to activate factors II, VII, IX, and X (as well as anti-clotting proteins C and S).

  15. A 7-year-old girl with happy personality and mental retardation undergoes karyotypic analysis that does not reveal a deletion of the expected UBE3A gene on chromosome 15. Which of the following findings via fluorescent in situ hybridization is most likely to confirm the diagnosis in this child?


    1. Maternal imprinting

    2. Maternal uniparental disomy

    3. Paternal imprinting

    4. Paternal uniparental disomy

      The correct answer is D.


      The diagnosis is Angelman syndrome (AS; aka “happy puppet” syndrome), caused usually by maternal mutation or deletion of the paternally imprinted UBE3A gene on chromosome 15. It can also be caused by paternal uniparental disomy.


      Imprinting is a normal genomic-wide process via which certain genes demonstrate a silencing of one allele from one parent.


      Maternal imprinting = allele coming from mom is silenced; therefore, only dad’s allele is expressed. Paternal imprinting = allele coming from dad is silenced; therefore, only mom’s allele is expressed.

      The UBE3A gene associated with Angelman syndrome is normally paternally imprinted, meaning even though two UBE3A alleles are inherited (one from each parent), only mom’s is normally expressed.   42. A couple has three children. Two of them have achondroplasia. Both parents are phenotypically normal. Which of the following best explains the disease pattern in this family?


    1. Germline mosaicism

    2. Heteroplasmy

    3. Imprinting

    4. Incomplete penetrance

    5. Variable expressivity

      The correct answer is A.


      This is a favorite USMLE question.


      Achondroplasia is an autosomal dominant disorder caused by mutations in the fibroblast growth factor receptor gene, FGFR3. Most cases of achondroplasia occur, however, when both parents are phenotypically normal. This is because of germline (gonadal) mosaicism.


      Germline (gonadal) mosaicism is when the spermatogonia or oogonia of one of the parents carry a mutation, but the somatic (body) cells do not. The result is that offspring can have an AD disorder despite the parents not being affected. Germline mosaicism is the answer classically for achondroplasia – i.e., the parents are normal but the child has the disease. The child will have 100% of his or her body cells affected because the original mutation was in the spermatogonia or oogonia of the parent, rather than occurring post-mitotically, as with somatic mosaicism. If the USMLE does not give achondroplasia, they will say two phenotypically normal parents have two children with an AD disorder, implying there is a germline mutation in one of the parents (usually the father).


      Incomplete penetrance is when not everyone with the diseased genotype bears the diseased phenotype (i.e., not everyone with the disease expresses it); this is sometimes referred to as “skipping a generation,” and also applies to the BRCA genes.


      Variable expressivity, in contrast, implies 100% penetrance – i.e., everyone with the disease expresses it in some form. The difference is the mere severity in which family members manifest the condition. NF1 is a classic example, where everyone with the disease has it, but whilst one family member might only present with axillary and groin freckling, another might develop pheochromocytoma and optic glioma. If the NBME/USMLE gives you a pedigree that looks like incomplete penetrance, but the diagnosis in the family is NF1, choose variable expressivity, not incomplete penetrance. A patient with minimal disease severity in NF1 might not report having it.


      Heteroplasmy is a phenomenon seen in mitochondrial disorders, an inheritance pattern characterized by strict maternal transmission (i.e., mothers transmit disease to sons and daughters; fathers never transmit disease), where varying amounts of diseased mitochondria are passed to the next generation, resulting in offspring with disease of varying severity. This is due to the mother’s oocytes carrying thousands of copies of mitochondrial DNA, where the oocytes differ in the ratios of diseased mitochondria they contain. Do not confuse this with variable expressivity, which instead refers to

      non-mitochondrial disorders, where a chromosomal mutation can result in varying severity of disease. Imprinting is when a gene is silenced coming from one parent. As mentioned earlier:

      Angelman syndrome = paternal imprinting + deleted/mutated allele from mom, OR paternal uniparental disomy (where both alleles can by all means be healthy but are silenced).


      Prader-Willi syndrome = maternal imprinting + deleted/mutated allele from dad, OR maternal uniparental disomy (where both alleles can by all means be healthy but are silenced).
